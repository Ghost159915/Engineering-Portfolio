Robotics Project

Micromouse

Autonomous maze-solving robot combining flood-fill exploration, shortest-path optimisation, and PID motion control.

Focus Mapping and Control
Stack C/C++, Sensors, Embedded
Status In progress

Overview

Micromouse is a classic competitive robotics challenge: a small autonomous robot must navigate an unseen 16 × 16 cell maze, find the centre, and then return on the fastest possible path. This project builds a complete micromouse from hardware up — covering PCB design, embedded firmware, maze-mapping algorithms, and speed-run motion control.

The project is currently in active development, with sensor integration and flood-fill mapping complete and PID speed-run tuning underway.

Hardware

  • Microcontroller: STM32 ARM Cortex-M4 running FreeRTOS for deterministic task scheduling across sensing, control, and communication threads.
  • Drive system: Dual DC micro gear-motors with quadrature encoders providing closed-loop wheel-speed feedback at 1 kHz.
  • Sensing: Four IR emitter–detector pairs (front, front-left, front-right, diagonal) for wall detection and lateral centering within cells.
  • Power: Single-cell LiPo with a 3.3 V / 5 V regulated supply; total platform mass kept below 100 g for low inertia in high-speed turns.

Software & Algorithms

  • Flood-fill exploration: During the first run, the robot builds a wall map cell-by-cell using its IR sensors. A flood-fill algorithm continuously updates a distance-to-goal score for every known cell, guiding the robot toward the centre while systematically uncovering unknown passages.
  • Shortest-path speed run: Once the centre has been reached, the complete wall map is used to compute the globally optimal route. On the speed-run, the robot follows this path at maximum speed, using diagonal traversal through open straights to cut corner-to-corner distance.
  • PID motion control: Independent PID loops govern straight-line speed (encoder feedback) and lateral wall-centering (IR differential). A separate turn-rate controller handles in-place and smooth-curve 90° and 180° turns, calibrated to minimise overshoot at competition speeds.

Status & Next Steps

Flood-fill mapping and slow-speed navigation are verified in simulation and on a physical half-size test maze. Current work focuses on PID gain tuning for high-speed straight runs and implementing smooth diagonal motion primitives for the speed-run phase.
